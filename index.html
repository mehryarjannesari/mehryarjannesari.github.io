Projects

Inertial Lift Forces on a Particle in Newtonian Fluid and Xanthan Gum Solutions

Mar. 2021 – July 2021

Instructor: Dr. A. Jafari

Inertial lift forces are the underlying reason for the inertial migration of particles across the microchannel cross-section. These forces are also significant in particle separation as they decide the particle trajectory in some Reynolds numbers. In this project, based on a particular approach, the inertial lift forces were numerically calculated for a particle in the Poiseuille flow of a Newtonian fluid and three different Xanthan gum solutions. Since these solutions show distinctive shear-thinning characteristics, the power-law model was utilized for representing their viscosity. It was observed that the shear-thinning behavior moves the particle equilibrium position toward the microchannel walls, thus introducing an additional degree of freedom to the system.


Stability Analysis of a Laminar Wall Jet in a Decelerating External Flow

Mar. 2021 – July 2021

Instructor: Prof. K. Sadeghy

Wall jets have many practical applications in the industry. In this project, spectral methods were used to perform a temporal stability analysis and determine the effect of the magnitude of decelerating external flow on the stability curve. The transformed Falkner-Skan equation was solved as both Boundary Value Problem (BVP) and Initial Value Problem (IVP) to obtain similarity profiles of velocity. A golden section optimization algorithm was developed to optimize the pressure gradient parameter. Then, the linearized Orr-Sommerfeld equation was discretized by Chebyshev polynomials. The results showed that the magnitude of decelerating external flow has a strong stabilization effect.


A Novel Bubble-driven Micromixer/Micropump Based on Thermal-inkjet Technology

Oct. 2020 – Mar. 2021

Instructor: Dr. V. Bazargan

Of paramount importance is mixing in microfluidics applications such as biological, chemical, and polymerization processes but is limited to the laminar nature of microfluidic flows. In this project, a shortened square-wave microchannel with a circular TIJ microheater located at the center of the fluids interface in the first turn of the channel was simulated. The generation of the bubble perturbs the interface between the fluids by creating vortexes around the microheater and also pushes the fluids to the outlet of the domain. The complex dynamic behavior of the thermal bubble was simplified by applying its initial pressure and volume based on the explosive vaporization phenomenon.


Analysis of the Flow through a Converging-Diverging Duct (Stenosis)

Sep. 2020 – Oct. 2020

Instructor: Dr. V. Bazargan

The flow of an incompressible fluid through an axisymmetric converging-diverging tube, which is a case for mild stenosis, was numerically simulated. The average pressure and flow rate over a cardiac cycle were considered as the inlet boundary conditions of the problem. Non-dimensionalized continuity and momentum equations were solved for blood as a Newtonian fluid based on Morgan and Young simplifications. Based on this approach, a nonlinear ordinary differential equation was solved for the centerline velocity as a function of stenosis shape. Then, the velocity and pressure profiles were obtained in terms of centerline velocity and stenosis height and length.


Two-dimensional Incompressible Laminar Navier-Stokes and Energy Equations in C++

Nov. 2019 – Jan. 2020

Instructor: Dr. A. Jalali

This project consists of two parts; at first, a lid-driven cavity flow with a horizontal movement of its top wall was solved. A SIMPLE algorithm along with finite-volume discretization was developed in which the momentum and pressure correction equations were solved by Gauss-Seidel scheme, and collocated grid arrangement was utilized for implementation of Rhie-Chow interpolation. The results were verified by solving the same problem with the equations of stream function and vorticity. In the second part, the thermal development of a flow in a channel in which the velocity is already fully-developed was scrutinized. The explicit and implicit Euler time advance schemes were developed from scratch, and the stability criteria of each method were addressed for different grid sizes.
